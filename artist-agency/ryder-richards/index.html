Ryder Richards, born in 1977, grew up in Roswell, New Mexico. He holds a Bachelor of Fine Arts from Texas Tech University with a minor in architecture. He earned a Master of Fine Arts from Texas Christian University. Ryder has studied art throughout the United States and in Italy, Spain, and Germany.

After opening and running two alternative art venues in Lubbock, Texas Richards became the Gallery Coordinator for Richland College in Dallas, Texas in late 2009. The gallery has received regional press attention as an experimental arts venue providing space for burgeoning artists exploring non-commercial concepts.

Richards is co-founder of the Ryder Jon Piotrs Nomadic Gallery, a traveling art gallery in the form of a 24’ Ryder moving truck, and Culture Laboratory, an internet based collective of American artists interested in the exchange of ideas and the social development of art in the 21st century. Both collaborations have spurred creative, social programming leading to Richards more recent open ended collaborative projects and membership with The Art Foundation in Dallas.

Known for his many artistic collaborations, Richards continues to explore art through object making and art as social experience. He is the recipient of several scholarships and awards for his achievements in art, including artist-in-residence programs at Roswell, New Mexico; Vermont Studio Center, Vermont; Hilmsen, Germany; Paducah, Kentucky and Portales, New Mexico. Writing has also become a facet of Richards art practice. Focused on exploratory exhibitions in the DFW region, Richards considers his writing communicative.

Ryder Richards Artist Statement

In 1906 William James states that “The possibility of violent death [is] the soul of all romance.”1 My work is an investigation of the socio-cultural and aesthetic influences that affect our decisions, especially as related to romance, violence, and architecture.

Born and raised in Roswell until the age of 15, many of my formative experiences and influences are derived from the region. Drawing inspiration from Cultures of Honor2 –cultural systems based on reputation as a means of survival— my work has moved increasing towards the subliminal influences that validate the replication of ideas within a complex social system.

With several facets to my practice and constantly expanding ideas, each project begins to take on it’s unique identity. I am best know for my process that involves, in part, the use of gunpowder, paper and/or wood construction. The patterns in my art are derived from decorative patterning found on rifle breeches or pistol grips. Creating compositions with these patterns, I “draw” by igniting gunpowder. The works embrace this violence as a method of creation, referencing both the seduction of the patterning and the possibility of destruction.

~ Ryder, 2012
1 “The Moral Equivalent of War” by William James, 1906
2 “Culture of Honor: The Psychology of Violence in the South” by Richard Nesbitt and Dov Cohen, 1996
